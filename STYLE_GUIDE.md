# Writing Style Guide — Measles: A Complete Learning Guide

All content in this app follows this guide.

## Tone and voice
- Write in the voice of an infectious disease epidemiologist and public health professor: historically informed, conversational, and direct.
- Blend narrative (history, policy, real-world scenarios) with clear explanations of the evidence.
- Sound like a knowledgeable, approachable expert teaching the public — not a marketing team.
- Be respectful but firm. When a claim is wrong or misleading, explain why, without insulting or mocking people.
- Keep an explicit public health and equity lens: talk about access, trust, history, and structural factors — not just "people won't listen."

## Reading level and clarity
- Target an 8th–10th grade reading level.
- Short to medium sentences. Avoid long, winding structures.
- Define any necessary jargon immediately in plain language.
- Use concrete examples and numbers for risk (e.g., "out of 100,000 people…") and say what the number means ("this is very rare," "this is a large drop in hospitalization risk").
- One main idea per paragraph.

## Banned language

AI-ish abstract nouns: tapestry, landscape, framework, interplay, nuance, dynamics, paradigm.

Promotional / brochure words: "rich tapestry," "nestled in the heart of," "boasts a stunning…," vibrant, bustling, picturesque, idyllic.

Puffery / importance-inflating: "plays a vital role," "cannot be overstated," "stands as a testament to," "enduring legacy," "indelible mark," "profound impact."

Narrator-as-analyst verbs (explaining what an action means instead of showing it): highlighting, underscoring, showcasing, emphasizing, "reflecting" (in the sense of "this underscores/reflects…").

Avoid generic closing lines and pseudo-profound, motivational-poster statements.

## Preferred style
- Plain, concrete language and specific examples instead of abstractions.
- When tempted to use a banned phrase, replace it with a specific number, detail, or plain description of what happened and why it matters.
- Prioritize clarity and specificity over "sounding profound."
